<1/> Breaking down the Poly.Network attack

The PolyNetwork hack last week was an interesting one because if equipped with the correct information, you can get a general technical idea of what took place. PolyNetwork is a decentralised finance (Defi) platform that is a collection of smart contracts (programs stored on the blockchain) that allow transactions between different blockchains. To understand the attack, you need to know how the networkâ€™s programs/contracts were running.

There were two contracts involved, the EthCrossChainData (the accountant), which stores all the other accounts, then there is the EthCrossChainManager (the accountantâ€™s boss). When a transaction is made, it goes through the Manager, whose job is to validate it and then send it over to the EthCrossChainData contract, which is owned by and can only execute actions received by the EthCrossChainManager. There were two flaws exploited in this attack. The first being that the EthCrossChainManager could tell the EthCrossChainData to take orders from another contract with the same privileges as the Manager. The second and one used by the hackers was that the Manager could be tricked into making the first scenario happen. With that, the attackers were able to switch the Manager to one controlled by them. This story involves cyber attacks (perfectly explained in this Twitter thread), crypto and a series of ongoing events after the attack. All things that can sum up the times we live in now.

This article explores HTTP3, the technology that will solve this problem. It's just part-one and goes in-depth in helping one understand: The problem is being solved. HTTP is just a collection of instructions/protocols used in every request. HTTP3 is an upgrade to the underlying one TCP. QUIC is TCP 2.0, bringing with it everything like streams and packets. It just does it smarter and can optimise not only your network calls but also forget about the failed ones. Security & Limitations. Lambda functions can be hard to manage. You have to take care of failures; sometimes, your instance will timeout; adding in and updating schedules or logging is not easy with constantly changing business requirements. AWS Step Functions is a low-code service that allows users to visually build workflows that are made up of a collection of Lambda functions. The service is based on state machines and tasks. A task being a state in a workflow, which represents a single unit of work that another AWS service performs. Where the state is needed, is when the service examines each step in your workflow to make that the application runs in order.
